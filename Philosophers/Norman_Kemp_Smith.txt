'''Norman Duncan Kemp Smith''', [[Fellow of the British Academy|FBA]], [[Fellow of the Royal Society of Edinburgh|FRSE]] (5 May 1872 – 3 September 1958) was a Scottish philosopher who was Professor of Psychology (1906–1914) and Philosophy (1914–1919) at [[Princeton University]] and was Professor of [[Logic]] and [[Metaphysics]] at the [[University of Edinburgh]] (1919–1945).<ref name="times">{{cite news |title=Obituary: Prof. N. Kemp Smith – Kantian scholar |work=[[The Times]] |page= 11 |date=  6 September 1958}}</ref>

Smith is noted for his 1929 English translation of [[Immanuel Kant]]'s ''[[Critique of Pure Reason]]'', which for a long time was considered the standard version.<ref>{{Cite book|last=Scruton|first=Roger|title=Kant, A Very Short Introduction|publisher=Oxford University Press|year=2001|isbn=978-0-19-280199-9|pages=133}}</ref><ref>{{cite book |last= |first= |title= Immanuel Kant's Critique of Pure Reason |translator= Norman Kemp Smith |publisher=Macmillan and Co. Limited |place=London |year= 1929|url= https://archive.org/details/in.ernet.dli.2015.207294/page/n9/mode/2up |via= [[Internet Archive]]}}</ref>

==Early life and education==
Norman Smith was born on 5 May 1872<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.humesociety.org/scholarship/scholars/kempsmith.asp|title=Norman Kemp Smith (1872-1958)|website=www.humesociety.org|access-date=2019-05-28}}</ref> in [[Dundee]], Scotland,<ref>{{cite book |last1=Porteous |first1=A. J. D. |url=https://archive.org/details/credibilityofdiv0000smit |title=The Credibility of Divine Existence: The Collected Papers of Norman Kemp Smith |date=1967 |publisher= Springer|isbn=9781349816552 |page=3 |language=en |chapter=Biographical Sketch: Norman Kemp Smith (1872–1958) |access-date=4 July 2017 |url-access=registration}}</ref> the son of a cabinet-maker on the Nethergate.<ref>Dundee Post Office Directory 1871</ref> He was educated in Dundee and then studied mental philosophy at the [[University of St. Andrews|University of St Andrews]], graduating with an MA with first-class honours in 1893.<ref name="Herald 4Sep1958">{{cite news |url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=JDY1AAAAIBAJ&pg=6015%2C512049 |title=Professor Norman Kemp Smith. A translator of Descartes |work=[[The Glasgow Herald]] |date=4 September 1958 |page=9 |access-date=15 May 2017}}</ref> He received his doctorate (PhD) in 1902.

==Career==
Smith lectured in philosophy and psychology at [[Princeton University]] from 1906 to 1916, and at the [[University of Edinburgh]] from 1919 until his retirement in 1945.{{citation needed|date=October 2024}} He was elected a Fellow of the [[Royal Society of Edinburgh]] in 1921. His proposers were [[Ralph Allan Sampson]], [[Thomas James Jehu]], [[Charles Glover Barkla]] and [[Charles Sarolea]].<ref>{{cite book|title=Biographical Index of Former Fellows of the Royal Society of Edinburgh 1783–2002|date=July 2006|publisher=The Royal Society of Edinburgh|isbn=0-902-198-84-X|url=https://www.royalsoced.org.uk/cms/files/fellows/biographical_index/fells_indexp2.pdf|access-date=27 July 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160304074135/https://www.royalsoced.org.uk/cms/files/fellows/biographical_index/fells_indexp2.pdf|archive-date=4 March 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref> In 1932 he delivered the [[List of Adamson Lectures|Adamson Lecture]]<ref>{{cite web|title=John Locke (1632-1704): the Adamson lecture for 1932, by Norman Kemp Smith|website=Catalogue, National Library of Australia|url=https://catalogue.nla.gov.au/Record/2246722}}</ref> of the [[Victoria University of Manchester]].

In 1938 he moved to 14 Kilgraston Road in south Edinburgh, a house designed by [[Sir Robert Matthew]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.ed.ac.uk/ppls/philosophy/about/history/norman-kemp-smith|title=Norman Kemp Smith|website=The University of Edinburgh|language=en|access-date=2019-05-28}}</ref>

His translation of Immanuel Kant's ''Critique of Pure Reason'' is often used as the standard English version of the text. His commentaries on the ''Critique'' are also well regarded, as are his works on [[David Hume]] and other philosophers. He was president of the [[Aristotelian Society]] from 1947 to 1948. A portrait by the Edinburgh artist [[Adam Bruce Thomson]] is held by the University of Edinburgh's Fine Art Collection.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://artuk.org/search/search/search/keyword:adam-bruce-thomson|title=Portrait of Norman Kemp Smith|access-date=20 February 2015}}</ref>

Kemp Smith died on 3 September 1958 in [[Edinburgh]].<ref name="Herald 4Sep1958"/>

==Family==

In 1910 he married Amy Kemp (d.1936), and thereafter became known as Norman Kemp Smith.<ref>[https://www.ed.ac.uk/ppls/philosophy/about/history/norman-kemp-smith Norman Kemp Smith | The University of Edinburgh], University of Edinburgh Philosophy Department web site. Retrieved 18 October 2023.</ref>

==Legacy==
The Kemp Smith Room in the [[University of Edinburgh|University of Edinburgh's]] Philosophy Department is named in his honour.<ref>{{cite book|title=Biographical Index of Former Fellows of the Royal Society of Edinburgh 1783–2002|date=July 2006|publisher=The Royal Society of Edinburgh|isbn=0-902-198-84-X|url=https://www.royalsoced.org.uk/cms/files/fellows/biographical_index/fells_indexp2.pdf|access-date=27 July 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160304074135/https://www.royalsoced.org.uk/cms/files/fellows/biographical_index/fells_indexp2.pdf|archive-date=4 March 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref>
