{{Short description|French philosopher and politician (1918–2004)}}
'''Joseph Adolphe Rovan''' (born '''Joseph Adolph Rosenthal''' in [[Munich]], Germany on  July 25, 1918, died July 27, 2004), was a French philosopher and politician, and is considered a spiritual father of post-war Europe. Initially born into the [[Jew]]ish faith, on Whitsunday 1944 he was received into the Catholic Church.

Rovan was active in the [[French Resistance]] during World War II and was awarded the [[Croix de guerre 1939–1945|Croix de Guerre]] and the [[Médaille de la Résistance]] for his services. In 1944, he was arrested by the [[Gestapo]] and survived 10 months in the [[Dachau concentration camp]]. It was during this time that he converted to [[Roman Catholicism|Catholicism]]. In 1945, after his return to France, he wrote in the magazine [http://www.esprit.presse.fr ''Esprit''] the article "L'Allemagne de nos mérites," where he suggested that the creation of a [[democracy|democratic]] Germany on the ruins of the [[Third Reich]] was the responsibility of the [[Allies of World War II|Allies]].

Rovan has also been awarded the [[Legion d'Honneur]], the [[Ordre National du Mérite]], the [[Federal Cross of Merit|German Order of Merit with Star]], and the [[Bavarian Order of Merit]]. He died in a swimming accident in France.
