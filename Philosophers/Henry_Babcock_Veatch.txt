'''Henry Babcock Veatch Jr.''' (September 26, 1911 &ndash; July 9, 1999) was an American philosopher.

==Life and career==
Veatch was born September 26, 1911, in [[Evansville, Indiana|Evansville]], [[Indiana]].  He attended [[Harvard University]], where he received his [[Bachelor of Arts|A.B.]] and [[Master of Arts|M.A.]] degrees and obtained a [[Ph.D.]] in 1937. Veatch came to the Philosophy Department at [[Indiana University]] as an instructor in 1937. He was named assistant professor in 1941 and full professor in 1952. While at Indiana University, Veatch received many awards and honors. In 1954, he became the first recipient of the Frederick Bachman Lieber Award for Distinguished  Teaching. He was popular with his students and was awarded the [[Sigma Delta Chi]] "Brown Derby" Award for most popular professor. In 1961, Veatch was named Distinguished Service Professor.<ref name="Veatchpapers">{{cite web |url=http://purl.dlib.indiana.edu/iudl/findingaids/archives/InU-Ar-VAA2654|title=Henry Babcock Veatch papers, 1941-1997|publisher=Archives Online at Indiana University}}</ref>

In 1965, Veatch left IU for [[Northwestern University]] where he remained until 1973. He then went to [[Georgetown University]], where he was philosophy department chair from 1973 to 1976.<ref name="Veatchpapers" /> Veatch also had visiting professorships at [[Colby College]], [[Haverford College]] and [[St. Thomas University (New Brunswick)|St. Thomas University]]. In 1983, he retired as a distinguished professor and returned to [[Bloomington (Indiana)|Bloomington]].<ref name="Veatchpapers" />

Veatch was active in the [[Episcopal Church (United States)|Episcopal Church]] and served as president of the [[American Catholic Philosophical Association]]. He served as president of the [[Metaphysical Society of America]] in 1961. In 1970&ndash;71 he served as president of the Western Division of the [[American Philosophical Association]]. He was a member of the [[Guild of Scholars of The Episcopal Church]].

Henry Veatch died in [[Bloomington (Indiana)|Bloomington]], [[Indiana]].  Indiana University maintains the archive of his collected papers (1941&ndash;1997).

==Philosophy==
Veatch was a major proponent of [[intellectualism]], an authority on [[Thomism|Thomistic philosophy]], and one of the leading neo-[[Aristotelianism|Aristotelian]] thinkers of his time.  He opposed such modern and contemporary developments as the "[[transcendental idealism|transcendental turn]]" and the "[[linguistic turn]]."  A staunch advocate of plain speaking and "[[Hoosier]]" common sense, in philosophy and elsewhere, he argued on behalf of [[philosophical realism|realist]] [[metaphysics]] and practical [[ethics]].<ref>Rocco Porecco and Ronald Duska, "Memorial Minutes: Henry Veatch 1911&ndash;1999," ''Proceedings and Addresses of the American Philosophical Association'', vol. 73, no. 2 (Nov/1999), pp. 31&ndash;2.</ref>

Veatch's most widely read book was ''Rational Man: A Modern Interpretation of Aristotelian Ethics'' (1962) which explicitly offered a rationalist counterpoint to [[William Barrett (philosopher)|William Barrett's]] well-known study in [[existentialism|existential philosophy]], ''[[Irrational Man]]'' (1958).
