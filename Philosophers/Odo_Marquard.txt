'''Odo Marquard''' (26 February 1928 – 9 May 2015) was a German [[philosophy|philosopher]]. He was a professor of philosophy at the [[University of Giessen]] from 1965 to 1993. In 1984 he received the [[Sigmund Freud Prize]] for Scientific Prose.

==Early life and education==
Odo Marquard was born in [[Słupsk|Stolp]], [[Farther Pomerania]]. He studied philosophy, German literature and theology, obtaining his doctorate at the [[University of Münster]] and his [[habilitation]] at the [[University of Freiburg]].{{sfn|University of Giessen|2015}} In Münster he studied under [[Joachim Ritter]], whose Ritter School he sometimes is considered a member of.{{sfn|Lübbe|2016|pp=117–118}} An even greater influence was [[Max Müller (Catholic intellectual)|Max Müller]], whom Marquard studied under in Freiburg, and his use of the philosophy of [[Edmund Husserl]] and [[Martin Heidegger]] to create a [[Phenomenology (philosophy)|phenomenological]] update of [[neo-scholasticism]].{{sfn|Lübbe|2016|pp=118–120}}

==Career==
From 1965 to 1993, Marquard held a chair for philosophy at the [[University of Giessen]], serving as [[Dean (education)|dean]] of the philosophical faculty. In 1982–1983 he was a [[fellow]] at the [[Berlin Institute for Advanced Study]]. From 1985 to 1987 he was the president of the General Society for Philosophy in Germany.{{sfn|University of Giessen|2015}}

In 1984 he was awarded the [[Sigmund Freud Prize]] for Scientific Prose by the [[Deutsche Akademie für Sprache und Dichtung]]. He was awarded the Erwin-Stein-Preis (1992), the [[Ernst-Robert-Curtius-Preis]] for essay writers (1996), the [[Hessian Cultural Prize]] for science (1997), the [[Hessian Order of Merit]] (1990) and two [[Order of Merit of the Federal Republic of Germany|Orders of Merit of the Federal Republic of Germany]]: the Cross of Merit 1. Class (1995) and the Great Cross of Merit (2008). In 1994, the year after he became professor [[emeritus]], he received an honorary doctorate from the [[University of Jena]].{{sfn|University of Giessen|2015}}

==Thought==
A proponent of philosophical [[hermeneutics]] and [[Philosophical skepticism|skepticism]], Marquards work focuses on aspects of human fallibility, contingency and finitude.<ref>{{Cite book|title=Farewell to Matters of Principle. Philosophical Studies|last=Marquard|first=Odo|publisher=Oxford University Press|year=1989|isbn=978-0-19-505114-8|location=New York/Oxford}}</ref> He rejected idealist, rationalist and universalist conceptions and defended philosophical [[Epistemological particularism|particularism]] and [[Pluralism (philosophy)|pluralism]].<ref>{{Cite book|title=In Defense of the Accidental. Philosophical Studies|last=Marquard|first=Odo|publisher=Oxford University Press|year=1991|isbn=978-0-19-507252-5|location=New York/Oxford}}</ref> His essay "[[In Praise of Polytheism]]" provoked discussion and controversy in Germany. In it, he promotes a "disenchanted return of [[polytheism]]" as a [[political theology]].{{sfn|Gladigow|2004|p=22}}

Criticized by [[Jürgen Habermas]] as a representative of German [[neoconservatism]],<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Habermas|first=Jürgen|date=1983|title=Neoconservative Culture Criticism in the United States and West Germany: An Intellectual Movement in Two Political Cultures|journal=Télos|volume=56}}</ref> his philosophy has been described as a form of [[liberal conservatism]]<ref>{{Cite book|title=Philosophie der Bürgerlichkeit. Die liberalkonservative Begründung der Bundesrepublik|last=Hacke|first=Jens|publisher=Vandenhoeck & Ruprecht|year=2006|isbn=978-3-525-36842-8|location=Göttingen}}</ref> with various parallels to [[postmodernism|postmodern]] thought and the work of [[Richard Rorty]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Allen|first=Barry|date=2013|title=Postmodern Pragmatism and Skeptical Hermeneutics: Richard Rorty and Odo Marquard|journal=Contemporary Pragmatism|volume=10|issue=1|pages=91–111|doi=10.1163/18758185-90000250}}</ref>
