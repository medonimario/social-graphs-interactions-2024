'''Mohammed Arkoun''' ({{langx|ar|محمد أركون}}; 1 February 1928 – 14 September 2010) was an Algerian scholar and thinker. He was considered to have been one of the most influential secular scholars in [[Islamic studies]] contributing to contemporary intellectual [[Liberalism and progressivism within Islam|Islamic reform]].<ref>[http://www.qantara.de/webcom/show_article.php/_c-478/_nr-262/_cmt-b03c7854f73d795c1485a51443e67b60/i.html Mohammed Arkoun:A Modern Critic of Islamic Reason]</ref> In a career of more than 30 years, he had been a critic of the tensions embedded in his field of study, advocating Islamic [[modernism]], [[secularism]], and [[humanism]]. During his academic career, he wrote his numerous books mostly in French, and occasionally in English and Arabic.

==Academic career==
Arkoun was born in 1928 in Taourirt Mimoun, a Berber village in [[Kabylie|Great Kabylia]] in northern [[Algeria]]. His family was traditional, religious and relatively poor. His father was a shopkeeper in Ain al-Arba'a, a wealthy French settlement in east of [[Oran]]. He attended primary school in his Berber-speaking home village until he was nine-years-old. As the eldest son, he was expected to learn his father's trade, while continuing to attend primary school.<ref name=":0">{{cite encyclopedia|encyclopedia=Encyclopaedia of Islam|title=Mohammed Arkoun|publisher=[[Brill Publishers|Brill]]|date=2015|doi=10.1163/1573-3912_ei3_COM_27338|edition= 3rd|url=https://referenceworks.brillonline.com/entries/encyclopaedia-of-islam-3/arkoun-mohammed-COM_27338|first=Ursula|last=Günther}}</ref> He studied at the Faculty of Literature of the university of [[Algiers]] and at the [[University of Paris|Sorbonne]] in [[Paris]] ([[Agrégation|agrégé]] in [[Arabic language]] and [[Arabic literature|Literature]], 1956 and [[Doctor of Philosophy|Ph.D.]], 1968). He established his academic reputation with his studies of the [[historian|history]] and philosophy of [[Ibn Miskawayh]]. As he began to consider how one might rethink Islam in the contemporary world, his questioning provided a counterpoint to the predominant interpretations of both the Muslim world and the non-Muslim West. As the editor of ''Arabica'', he broadened the journal's scope, and played a significant role in shaping Western-language scholarship on Islam.{{Citation needed|date=February 2017}} He authored numerous books in French, English, and Arabic, including: ''Rethinking Islam'' ([[Boulder, Colorado]], 1994), ''L'immigration: défis et richesses'' (Paris, 1998) and ''The Unthought in Contemporary Islamic Thought'' (London, 2002).<ref name="TIO20100917">{{cite web|url=http://www.theismaili.org/cms/1087/Passing-of-influential-professor-of-Islamic-Studies-leaves-a-gap-that-will-be-impossible-to-fill |title=Passing of influential professor of Islamic Studies leaves 'a gap that will be impossible to fill' |date=2010-09-17 |publisher=TheIsmaili.org |access-date=2010-09-20}}</ref> His shorter studies appeared in many academic journals; his works have been translated into several languages.

He was decorated as a Commander of the French [[Legion of Honour|Legion of Honor]] in 2004,<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://www.cairn.info/les-vies-de-mohammed-arkoun--9782130631200-page-327.htm|title=Les vies de Mohammed Arkoun|last=Arkoun|first=Sylvie|publisher=[[Presses Universitaires de France|PUF]]|year=2014|pages=327–340|chapter=Mohammed Arkoun, palais de l’Élysée, 17 septembre 2004|series=Hors collection |isbn=978-2-13-063120-0 }}</ref> before that a Chevalier and Officer in 1984 and 1996, respectively.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.leconomiste.com/article/arkoun-hommage-au-doyen-de-la-pensee-islamique|title=Arkoun: Hommage au doyen de la pensée islamique|last=Belkhayat|first=Nadia|work=[[L'Économiste]]|issue=3363}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.libe.ma/Concours-international-sur-l-oeuvre-de-Mohammed-Arkoun_a37957.html|title=Concours international sur l'œuvre de Mohammed Arkoun|date=2013-05-10|work=[[Libération (Morocco)|Libération]]}}</ref> In 2001, Arkoun was asked to deliver the [[Gifford Lectures]], which enable a notable scholar to contribute to the advancement of [[theology|theological]] and philosophical thought.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2014-08-13 |title=Mohammed Arkoun |url=https://giffordarchives.org/lecturers/mohammed-arkoun |access-date=2024-09-09 |website=The Gifford Lectures |language=en}}</ref>

[[File:Bibliothèque_Mohammed-Arkoun,_Paris_6_July_2015.jpg|upright=1.35|thumb|Mohammed Arkoun Library, Paris]]
Arkoun taught at the [[Lumière University Lyon 2|Lyon 2 University]] (1969–1972), as a professor, and at the [[Paris 8 University]], and at the [[University of Paris III: Sorbonne Nouvelle|New Sorbonne University of Paris]] (1972–1992). He was a Fellow at [[Wissenschaftskolleg zu Berlin|Wissenschaftskolleg in Berlin]] (1986–1987 and 1990) and at the [[Institute for Advanced Study]] in [[Princeton, New Jersey]], U.S.A (1992–1993), visiting professor at [[University of California, Los Angeles]] (1969), [[Princeton University]] (1985), [[Temple University]], the University of Louvain-la-Neuve, [[Wallonia]], Belgium, (1977–1979), the Pontifical Institute of Arabic Studies in [[Rome]] and the [[University of Amsterdam]] (1991–1993) and served as a jury member for the [[Aga Khan Award for Architecture]]. At the time of his death he was [[Emeritus Professor]] at La Sorbonne as well as Senior Research Fellow and member of the Board of Governors of [[Institute of Ismaili Studies|The Institute of Ismaili Studies]] <small>[http://www.iis.ac.uk (IIS)]</small>, At IIS, he has taught various graduate courses including unthought in contemporary Islamic thought, rethinking Islam, contemporary challenges of Muslim world and traditions for almost a decade.<ref name="IIS20100916">{{cite web |url=http://iis.ac.uk/view_article.asp?ContentID=111895 |title=In Memory of Professor Mohammed Arkoun |date=2010-09-16 |publisher=The Institute of Ismaili Studies |access-date=2010-09-20}}</ref> He appeared on numerous occasions on French TV and magazines, on [[Berbère Radio Télévision|Berbère Télévision]] speaking in [[Kabyle language|Kabyle]], his mother tongue, and on [[Al Jazeera Media Network|Al Jazeera]] speaking in Arabic.

Arkoun died on the evening of September 14, 2010, in [[Paris]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.ennaharonline.com/en/news/4705.html |title=Death in Paris of Mohamed Arkoun |date=2010-09-15 |access-date=2010-09-20 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100919153708/http://www.ennaharonline.com/en/news/4705.html |archive-date=2010-09-19 }}</ref>

==Thought==
[[File:Mehdi Khalaji3.jpg|right|thumb|Mohamed Arkoun with [[Mehdi Khalaji]] (Paris, 2008).]]

Arkoun advocated a radical paradigm shift that would allow for the rethinking of Islam as a cultural and religious system and subvert ideological and dogmatic constructs with hegemonic claims. He was committed to contribute to an archaeology of the hidden, repressed, and marginalised elements of Islam, in order to uncover, and set free, what he called, 'the exhaustive tradition' of Islam. Most of his work was written in French rather than Arabic. In order to counteract the philological and historical bias of traditional Islamic studies, he advocated what he called “applied Islamology”—following Roger Bastide's concept of “applied anthropology.” Applied Islamology aims to establish a “disciplinary space between political and historical sciences” (Arkoun, The answers, 25), taking into consideration elements of the ''courte'' and ''longue durée'', as well as contemporary social factors. Arkoun developed an inclusive approach which seeks to deal with Islamic tradition in its entirety, including elements characterised by the religious leaders as heterodox, and therefore marginalised and repressed. He has adopted a multifaceted and holistic approach which looks between traditional dogma and axioms.<ref name=":0" />
