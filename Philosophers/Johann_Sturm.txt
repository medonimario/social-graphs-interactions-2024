'''Johann Christoph Sturm''' (3 November 1635 – 26 December 1703)<ref name="google">{{cite book|title=A New and General Biographical Dictionary|author1=Tooke, W.|author2=Beloe, W.|author3=Nares, R.|date=1798|publisher=G. G. and J. Robinson|page=[https://archive.org/details/newgeneralbiogra14took/page/221 221]}}</ref> was a German [[philosopher]], professor at [[University of Altdorf]] and founder of a short-lived scientific academy known as the [[Collegium Curiosum]], based on the model of the Florentine [[Accademia del Cimento]].<ref name=EB14>{{cite encyclopedia |year=1930 |title=Academies: Scientific Academies |encyclopedia=Encyclopaedia Britannica |edition=14|volume=1|page=81 |language=en}}</ref> He edited two volumes of the academy's proceedings under the title ''Collegium Experimentale'' (1676 and 1685).<ref name=EB14/> In 1670, he translated the works of Archimedes into German.<ref name="google"/>

Sturm is the author of ''Physica Electiva'' (1697), a book that criticized [[Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz]] and prompted him to publish a [[rebuke]]. Sturm's critique was aimed at Leibniz's view that [[Nature]] and/or its constituent parts possess some creative force of their own. This criticism was partly [[theological]], in that Sturm claimed Leibniz's view of Nature undermined the sovereignty of the [[Christian God]].<ref>Gottfried Leibniz, Philosophical Papers and Letters, ed. by Leroy Loemker, (Dordrecht, Holland: D. Reidel Publishing, 1969) 499-508.</ref>
