'''Edward Caird''' {{post-nominals|country=GBR|FRSE|FBA}} ({{IPAc-en|k|ɛər|d}}; 23 March 1835 – 1 November 1908) was a Scottish [[philosopher]]. He was a holder of [[LLD]], [[Doctor of Civil Law|DCL]], and [[DLitt]].

==Life==
[[File:Edward Caird Vanity Fair 4 April 1895.jpg|thumb|{{center|Caird as caricatured by Spy ([[Leslie Ward]]) in [[Vanity Fair (British magazine)|Vanity Fair]], April 1895.}}]]

The younger brother of the theologian [[John Caird (theologian)|John Caird]], he was the son of engineer John Caird, the proprietor of [[Caird & Company]],<ref>{{cite web |url = http://www.rse.org.uk/fellowship/fells_indexp1.pdf |title=Former RSE Fellows 1783–2002 |publisher=[[Royal Society of Edinburgh]] |access-date=19 September 2010 |url-status=dead |archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20061004113545/http://www.rse.org.uk/fellowship/fells_indexp1.pdf |archive-date = 4 October 2006 }}</ref> born at [[Greenock]] in [[Renfrewshire (historic)|Renfrewshire]], and educated at [[Greenock Academy]] and the Universities of [[University of Glasgow|Glasgow]] and [[University of Oxford|Oxford]] (B.A. 1863). He was a Fellow and Tutor of [[Merton College, Oxford|Merton College]] from 1864 to 1866.<ref name="MCreg">{{cite book|editor1-last=Levens|editor1-first=R.G.C.|title=Merton College Register 1900–1964|date=1964|publisher=Basil Blackwell|location=Oxford|page=6}}</ref><ref>Boucher, D., [[Raymond Geuss|Geuss, R.]], & [[Quentin Skinner|Skinner, Q.]], eds., ''The British Idealists'' (Cambridge: [[Cambridge University Press]], 1997), [https://books.google.com/books?id=E4_MpkL6ue4C&pg=PR36 p. xxxvi].</ref>{{rp|xxxvi}}

In 1866, he was appointed to the [[Professor of Moral Philosophy, Glasgow|Chair of Moral Philosophy]] at [[University of Glasgow|Glasgow]], which he held until 1893. In that year he became [[Master (college)|Master]] of [[Balliol College, Oxford|Balliol College]], from which he retired in 1907. In 1894 he was made an Honorary Fellow of Merton College.<ref name="MCreg" />

He was elected an Honorary Fellow of the [[Royal Society of Edinburgh]] in 1900.

In May 1902 he was at [[Caernarfon|Carnavon]] to receive the honorary degree [[D.Litt.]] (Doctor of Letters) from the [[University of Wales]] during the ceremony to install the Prince of Wales (later King [[George V]]) as Chancellor of that university.<ref>{{cite newspaper The Times |title=The Royal visit to Wales |date=5 May 1902 |page=10 |issue=36759 }}</ref>

He was a founder member of the [[Glasgow and West of Scotland Association for Women's Suffrage]],<ref>{{cite book |last=King |first=Elspeth |title = The Scottish Women's Suffrage Movement / ... compiled by Elspeth King to accompany the Government sponsored 'Right to Vote' exhibition organised to commemorate the 50th anniversary of the Representation of the People Act, from 9th September- 7th October 1978, Peoples Palace Museum, Glasgow Green |year=1978 |publisher=People's Palace Museum |location=Glasgow }}</ref> alongside his wife, Caroline.

The philosopher [[John Watson (philosopher)|John Watson]] was among his pupils at the University of Glasgow.<ref>{{cite web |url = http://www.giffordlectures.org/Author.asp?AuthorID=176 |title = Gifford Lecture Series – Biography – John Watson |access-date=2011-09-05 |url-status=dead |archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20110914052909/http://www.giffordlectures.org/Author.asp?AuthorID=176 |archive-date=14 September 2011 }}</ref>

He died in [[Oxford]] on 1 November 1908 and was buried there in St Sepulchres Cemetery.<ref>{{Cite book |url=https://www.royalsoced.org.uk/cms/files/fellows/biographical_index/fells_indexp1.pdf |title=Former Fellows of The Royal Society of Edinburgh, 1783–2002: Part 1 (A–J) |author=C D Waterston |author2=A Macmillan Shearer |publisher=[[Royal Society of Edinburgh]] |isbn=090219884X |date=July 2006 |access-date=18 September 2015 |archive-date=24 January 2013 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130124115814/http://www.royalsoced.org.uk/cms/files/fellows/biographical_index/fells_indexp1.pdf |url-status=dead}}</ref>

Caird was a [[Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel|Hegelian]] idealist and was an important contributor to the [[British idealism|British idealist]] movement.<ref>Mander, W. J. (2014). ''The Oxford Handbook of British Philosophy in the Nineteenth Century''. Oxford University Press. p. 560. {{ISBN|978-0-19-959447-4}}</ref><ref>Brown, Stuart; Collinson, Diane; Wilkinson, Robert. (1996). ''Biographical Dictionary of Twentieth-Century Philosophers''. Routledge. [https://books.google.com/books?id=UZQqBgAAQBAJ&pg=PT121 p. 121]. {{ISBN|0-415-06043-5}}</ref>{{rp|121}}

==Family==
He married Caroline Frances Wylie in 1867. They had no children.<ref>Anon., ''Who was Who: A Companion to "Who's Who"'' (London: [[A & C Black]], 1967), p. 111.</ref>
