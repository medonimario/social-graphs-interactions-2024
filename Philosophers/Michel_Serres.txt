'''Michel Serres''' ({{IPA|fr|miʃɛl sɛʁ|lang}}; 1 September 1930 – 1 June 2019) was a French [[philosopher]], theorist and writer. His works explore themes of science, time and death, and later incorporated [[prose]].

==Life and career==
The son of a bargeman, Serres entered France's naval academy, the [[École Navale]], in 1949 and the [[École Normale Supérieure]] in 1952. He [[Agrégation|aggregated]] in 1955, having studied [[philosophy]]. He spent the next few years as a naval officer before finally receiving his [[doctorate]] ([[doctorat ès lettres]]) in 1968 from the [[University of Paris]] (with a thesis titled ''Le Système de Leibniz et ses modèles mathématiques''), and began teaching in 1969 at the [[University of Paris I]].

As a child, Serres witnessed firsthand the violence and devastation of war. "I was six for my first dead bodies," he told [[Bruno Latour]].<ref>''Conversations on Science, Culture, and Time: Michel Serres Interviewed by [[Bruno Latour]]'', University of Michigan Press, 1995, p. 2.</ref> These formative experiences led him consistently to eschew [[scholarship]] based upon models of war, suspicion, and [[criticism]].

Serres served as a professor of French at [[Stanford University]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.stanford.edu/dept/DLCL/cgi-bin/web/people/faculty|title=Stanford faculty|access-date=20 January 2012|archive-date=17 February 2014|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140217123311/http://www.stanford.edu/dept/DLCL/cgi-bin/web/people/faculty|url-status=dead}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.stanford.edu/dept/DLCL/cgi-bin/web/people/michel-serres|title=Stanford faculty webpage|access-date=20 January 2012|archive-date=16 January 2014|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140116134852/http://www.stanford.edu/dept/DLCL/cgi-bin/web/people/michel-serres|url-status=dead}}</ref> He earned a reputation as a spell-binding lecturer and as the author of remarkably beautiful and enigmatic prose so reliant on the sonorities of French that it is considered practically untranslatable.{{cn|date=May 2024}} He took as his subjects such diverse topics as the mythical [[Northwest Passage]], the concept of the [[parasite]], and the [[Space Shuttle Challenger disaster|explosion of the Space Shuttle Challenger]]. More generally Serres was interested in developing a [[philosophy of science]] which does not rely on a [[metalanguage]] in which a single account of science is privileged and regarded as accurate. To do this he relied on the concept of [[translation]] between accounts rather than settling on one as authoritative. For this reason, Serres has relied on the figure of [[Hermes]] (in his earlier works) and [[angel]]s (in more recent studies) as messengers who translate (or map) back and forth between domains (i.e., between maps).{{cn|date=May 2024}}

He was an influence on intellectuals such as [[Bruno Latour]], [[Robert Pogue Harrison]], and [[Jonathan Bate]]. Perhaps his most enduring book is ''Le contrat naturel'' (1990), a prescient work addressing the need for philosophy to address the climate crisis. 

In an interview with [[Hans Ulrich Obrist]], Serres expressed interest in the [[emergence]] of a new [[political philosophy]] that addresses the [[digital content|digital]] context of the 21st century, "I think that out of this place of no law that is the Internet there will soon emerge a new law, completely different from that which organized our old metric space."<ref>{{cite web|url=http://032c.com/2014/michel-serres/|title=MICHEL SERRES|last=032c.com|access-date=17 July 2014|archive-date=23 July 2014|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140723233035/http://032c.com/2014/michel-serres/|url-status=live}}</ref>

Serres was a vocal enthusiast for [[free access|freely accessible]] knowledge, especially [[Wikipedia]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://framablog.org/2007/02/27/wikipedia/|title=Quand l'académicien Michel Serres valide Wikipédia|first=Alexis|last=Kauffmann|date=27 February 2007|website=Framablog|access-date=8 March 2024|archive-date=3 December 2012|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121203044634/http://www.framablog.org/index.php/post/2006/10/19/wikipedia|url-status=live}}</ref>

He died on 1 June 2019, at the age of 88.<ref>{{Cite news|url = https://www.lemonde.fr/disparitions/article/2019/06/01/le-philosophe-et-academicien-michel-serres-est-mort_5470322_3382.html|title = Le philosophe et académicien Michel Serres est mort|newspaper = Le Monde.fr|date = June 2019|access-date = 1 June 2019|archive-date = 5 June 2019|archive-url = https://archive.today/20190605082721/https://www.lemonde.fr/disparitions/article/2019/06/01/le-philosophe-et-academicien-michel-serres-est-mort_5470322_3382.html|url-status = live}}</ref>

==Honours and recognition==
In 1990, Serres was elected to the [[Académie française]], in recognition of his position as one of France's most prominent intellectuals. 

In 2012, Serres was awarded the [[Meister Eckhart Prize]] and in 2013 he was awarded the [[Dan David Prize]].
