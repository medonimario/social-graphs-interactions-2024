'''Ota Weinberger''' (20 April 1919 – 30 January 2009) was a Czech-Austrian [[jurist]], [[legal philosopher]], and [[logician]]. He was the [[Professor]] of Legal Science at the [[Faculty of Law of the University of Graz|University of Graz]] and the [[Charles University|University of Prague]].<ref name="who1">{{cite web|title=Ota Weinberger|url=https://www.ae-info.org/ae/Member/Weinberger_Ota|publisher=[[Academia Europaea]]|access-date=2023-03-28}}</ref>

== Biography ==
Weinberger was born in [[Brno]], [[Czechoslovakia]] on 20 April 1919 into an assimilated Jewish family of intellectuals. He was respected in the professional world as a logician and legal theorist who became known to a broad public beyond the academic framework as a political admonisher. For a long time, he suffered greatly from the political conditions in his homeland. As a [[Jew]], he was first persecuted by the [[Third Reich]]. He was initially unable to complete his [[Legal education|law studies]] at [[Masaryk University]] in his hometown and spent four years in [[concentration camp]]s. After the war, he completed his studies and entered the court service.<ref name=Bio1>{{cite web|title=''Ota Weinberger ist tot''|url= http://www.kleinezeitung.at/steiermark/graz/graz/1757683/index.do |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140923150838/http://www.kleinezeitung.at/steiermark/graz/graz/1757683/index.do|archive-date=2014-09-23 |publisher=Kleine Zeitung|access-date=2023-03-28|date=2009-02-02|language=de}}</ref><ref name="who3">{{cite journal|title=Ota Weinberger's conception of democracy: reconstructing an unexplored political theory|url=https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11212-022-09528-4|first=Marián|last= Sekerák|journal=Studies in East European Thought|publisher=[[Springer Publishing Company|Springer]]|date=2022|doi=10.1007/s11212-022-09528-4 |s2cid=254619077 |access-date=2023-03-28|doi-access=free}}</ref><ref name="who5">{{cite journal|title=Ota Weinberger: In Memoriam|url=https://heinonline.org/HOL/LandingPage?handle=hein.journals/raju22&div=37&id=&page=|first=Peter|last= Koller|journal=Ratio Juris|date=2009| volume=22 | issue=3 | pages=416–420 | doi=10.1111/j.1467-9337.2009.00432.x |access-date=2023-03-28}}</ref>

As a [[Liberal democracy|democrat and liberal thinker]], he refused to join the [[Czechoslovak Communist Party]] and was subsequently repressed by those in power. In the years 1953–1956, he had to work as a [[locksmith]] for political reasons.<ref name=Bio1/> Despite the most adverse circumstances, he managed to embark on an academic career as a Professor at the [[Charles University in Prague]], where he taught [[logic]] between 1956 and 1968.<ref name="who1"/><ref name="who3"/><ref name="who5"/>

In meantime, he defended his [[PhD dissertation]] at the [[Charles University|Charles University in Prague]] named ''Studie k logice normativních vět: Proč nemá smyslu mluvit o pravdivosti normativních vět. Dvě oblasti logiky normativních vět. Theorie důslekových vztahů a podmíněná normativní věta. Několik poznámek k normativni interpretaci systémů K1 a K2 J. Kalinowského''. In 1963, Weinberger became ''Docent'' (associate professor) at the same university.<ref name="who3"/><ref name="who5"/>

During the [[Prague Spring]] in 1968, Weinberger was in Vienna at the [[World Congress of Philosophy]] and never returned to his homeland. Only after the collapse of communism was he rehabilitated in the [[Czech Republic]]. Weinberger died on 30 January 2009, after a long and serious illness.<ref name=Bio1/><ref name="who5"/>

== Thoughts ==
In his PhD dissertation, Weinberger defends the applicability of logic to norms or normative propositions respectively. He created a pioneer work of the then-new discipline of [[deontic logic]] he preferred the term ''logic of norms''.<ref name="who5"/>
